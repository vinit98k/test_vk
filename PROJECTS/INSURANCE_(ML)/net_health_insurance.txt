[Problem Statement]
A key challenge for the insurance industry is to charge each customer an appropriate premium for the risk they represent. The ability to predict a correct claim amount has a significant impact on insurer's management decisions and financial statements. Predicting the cost of claims in an insurance company is a real-life problem that needs to be solved in a more accurate and automated way. Several factors determine the cost of claims based on health factors like BMI, age, smoker, health conditions and others. Insurance companies apply numerous techniques for analyzing and predicting health insurance costs.

[Data Collection]
"To train our machine learning model, we gathered a comprehensive dataset from various insurance providers. The dataset contained a wide range of features, including patient demographics, medical history, diagnosis codes, treatment details, healthcare provider information, and previous insurance claim amounts. We also collected data on external factors like economic indicators, regional health trends, and medical inflation rates to enhance the model's performance."

[Data Preprocessing]
"Data preprocessing played a crucial role in this project. We performed various steps, including data cleaning to handle missing values and outliers, feature engineering to create new informative features, and data normalization to ensure that all the features were on the same scale. Additionally, we split the data into training and testing sets to evaluate our model's performance accurately."

[Model Selection]
"Our objective was to build a regression model that could predict continuous values for net health insurance claims. After evaluating several algorithms, we selected three candidate models for comparison: Linear Regression, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting. These models were chosen due to their proven effectiveness in handling regression tasks and their ability to capture complex relationships within the data."

[Model Training]
"We trained each of the three models on the training dataset. During this process, we fine-tuned hyperparameters using techniques like cross-validation to optimize model performance. We also used techniques like regularization to prevent overfitting and ensure generalization to unseen data."

[Model Evaluation]
"To evaluate the performance of our models, we used several metrics such as mean squared error (MSE), mean absolute error (MAE), and R-squared (R2). We compared the results of each model on the testing dataset to assess their accuracy in predicting net health insurance claims."

[Results]
"After thorough evaluation, we found that the Gradient Boosting model outperformed both Linear Regression and Random Forest in terms of accuracy. It provided the lowest MSE, MAE, and the highest R2 value, indicating its superior predictive capabilities."

[Conclusion]

The Gradient Boosting model demonstrated the highest accuracy in forecasting claim amounts.
